Introduction

The case for UG

  • What is the case for a 'Universal Grammar'?
    • Something that cannot be learned, but present nonetheless.
    • Famous argument: poverty of the stimulus.

The cat who is sitting is staring at the dog who is running.

  • Necessary evidence not present in the input.

Morphological evidence for UG

What is Suppletion?

  • A lexical item has two or more phonolically unrelated forms.
  • Use determined by morphological context.
  • Examples:

    • person~people

      • *persons, change determined by plurality

    gut~besser

      • *guter, changed determined by comparative

    Adjectival Suppletion

    • Bobaljik (2012) looks at patterns of suppletion in adjectives, for comparative and superlative degrees.

    Attested patterns of adjectival suppletion
    Positive Comparative Superlative Pattern
    smart smarter smartest AAA
    good better best ABB
    bonus melior optimus ABC

    Missing Patterns

    • But, there are two patterns that are not attested:

    Positive Comparative Superlative Pattern
    good better goodest ABA
    good gooder best AAB

    Generalisations

    1. (*ABA): If the comparative is suppletive, then the superlative is also suppletive.
    2. (*AAB): For the superlative to be suppletive, then comparative must also be.
    • The second is not of interest here.
    • AAB patterns in suppletion are found in other areas
    • *ABA will be the focus of the remainder of the talk.

    Robustness

    • How robust is this?
      • Very.
      • Data drawn from over 300 languages, representing 40+ families.
      • Total dataset = 100 distinct cognate triples
      • The use of cognate triples ensures that English good~better~best and Dutch goed~beter~best are treated as the same data point.

    Summary

    • ABA and AAB are logically possible patterns.
    • There is no a priori reason why they shouldn't exist.
    • The patterns are clearly learnable, so why do they not arise?

    Explanation: Bobaljik

    • Adjective, Comparative and Superlative are in a containment relation.
    • The superlative cannot appear without the comparative.
    • Sprl → Comp

    Pronominal Number

    Number

    • Number shows similar typological behaviour to gradation (Corbett 2004), suggesting similar containment patterns.
    • At least a three-way number distinction is necessary to test *ABA.
    • Concentrate on singular–plural–dual
      • singular = one entity
      • dual = two entities
      • plural = more than two entities

    Explanation

    • Simple version, see Smith et al (2019) for details.
    • The features that make up the plural are contained within those that make up the dual.
    • Plural = [−Sing]
    • Dual = [−Sing, −Aug]
    • Any item suppletive for plural will also be suppletive for dual.

    Discussion

    • Again, ABA patterns are ruled out.
      • More evidence that categories in morphology are consistently structurally complex.
    • AAB patterns are seen
      • No general ban against AAB (cf. Moskal 2018).
    • There is a plausible semantic story here.
      • [±Sing] divides the referent into singulars and non-singulars.
      • [±Aug] effectively classifies minimal vs. non-minimal groups.

    Pronominal case

    Morphological Case

    • Case encodes the roles that items play in sentences.
    • In German (usually):
      • Nominative = subject
      • Accusative = (direct) object
      • Dative = goal/indirect object
    • Case has been argued to be complex (Caha 2009).

    Bulding the survey

    • We looked for patterns suppletion for case in pronouns (we~us).
    • In order to be instructive, languages need to show 3+ case distinctions.
    • We ordered the cases on a hierarchy following Marantz (1991):
      • Nominative–Accusative–Dative
      • Absolutive–Ergative–Dative
      • Since this doesn't matter, I'll use Case 1, Case 2, Case 3

    • More complex cases like Dative contain less context cases like Accusative, which in turn contain cases like nominative.

    Discussion

    • ABA patterns are not seen in case morphology.
    • Clear restrictions on suppletion in pronouns though.
    • Important: There is no semantic justification for this.
      • Remarkably few theories of case would attribute a semantics to case.

    General Discussion

    Consequences

    • Major finding: *ABA in case, number
      • Also: adjectives (Bobaljik 2012), clusivity (Moskal 2018), possession (van Baal and Don) and (germanic) verbs (Andersson 2018).
    • Note: This is a complete absence of ABA patterns.
    • Points towards a universal grammar explanation.
      • No clear learning path based on positive evidence to *ABA.
      • We cannot resort to semantics, given that *ABA holds in case.

    Is there an alternative?

    • Our account rests on decomposing pronouns, in order to make rules.
    • But then we need to tolerate some readjustment.
    • It's often not clear where the segmentation should be.
    Case AAA vs ABB
    Case 1 Case 2 Case 3 Language
    zi zad zas Rutul AAA
    bi namaigi nadada Khalkha ABB

    Alternative

    • In the absence of clear evidence, assume that children learn irregularity holistically.
    • Khalkha: ours vs. the alternative:
      • 1 ⇔ b
      • 1 ⇔ na / __ acc
      • [sg,nom] ⇔ -i
      • [sg,acc] ⇔ maigi
      • [sg,dat] ⇔ dada
      • [1sg, nom] ⇔ bi
      • [1sg, acc] ⇔ namaigi
      • [1sg, dat] ⇔ nadada

    Alternative

    • The alternative seems simpler.
    • Morphology is messy, often very hard to decompose.
    • Alternative: storage of clear, regular rules + deviations from these.

    Alternative

    • However: remember that *ABA is a complete absence.
    • Without containment of categories, it's hard to see how to derive *ABA.
    • Hypothetical:
      • √good ⇔ good
      • √better ⇔ better / __ comp
      graph TD classDef empty fill:#ffffff,stroke-width:0px; A[Adj] --- B["#8730;GOOD"] A --- C[Sprl] class A,B,C,D,E,F,G empty;
